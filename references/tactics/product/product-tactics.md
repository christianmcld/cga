# Product Tactics — Viral Loops, Referrals, Engineered Word-of-Mouth

Source: Bronson Taylor, Growth Hacking Playbook Module 3

---

## Network Invitations

- Prompt users to invite contacts **immediately upon registration** (when engagement and excitement are highest)
- LinkedIn's foundational growth mechanism: on signup, users were prompted to upload address books and invite connections
- The invitation IS the product experience — connecting with people is why you joined
- Key: the invitation must feel like it benefits the sender, not just the platform

## Social Sharing

Must satisfy Jonah Berger's STEPPS framework to spread:

1. **Social Currency** — sharing makes the user look good (smart, funny, in-the-know)
2. **Triggers** — connected to everyday cues that remind people to talk about it
3. **Emotion** — high-arousal emotions (awe, anxiety, anger) drive sharing; low-arousal (sadness, contentment) kills it
4. **Public** — the product or behavior must be visible to others (Apple's "Sent from iPhone," Hotmail's email footer)
5. **Practical Value** — useful enough that people share to help others
6. **Stories** — wrapped in a narrative people want to retell (the product is embedded in the story, not bolted on)

If your sharing mechanism doesn't check at least 3-4 of these, it won't spread.

## API Integrations

- Connect your product to established platforms where users already spend time
- **Nike + Facebook**: runners share goals and achievements to their feed — Nike gets free distribution, users get social validation
- The integration must create value for both platforms and the user — parasitic integrations get shut down (see: Zynga/Facebook, early Twitter API abuse)
- Target platforms where your users already have audiences

## Referral Incentives

### Design Principles

- Reward must be **valuable to the user** AND **low-cost for the company to provide**
- Best referral rewards are product-native: more storage, extended trial, premium features, credits
- Cash rewards work but create mercenary behavior — product rewards create loyal users
- Double-sided incentives (both referrer and referee get value) outperform one-sided

### Dropbox Case Study

- **100K to 4M users in 15 months** — 3,900% growth
- Referral reward: 500MB extra storage for both referrer and invitee
- Cost to Dropbox per referral: essentially zero (storage is cheap at scale)
- Value perceived by user: high (storage was the core constraint)

### Critical Implementation Details

- Referral placed as the **FINAL step in onboarding** — user hits it at peak excitement, right after experiencing the product's value
- Framed as **"Get more space"** not "Invite your friends" — the CTA is about the user's benefit, not the company's growth goal
- Progress bar showed how much space you'd earned — gamified the referral process
- Made sharing dead simple: one-click email, Facebook, Twitter, unique referral link

## Word-of-Mouth by Design

Products that generate organic WOM share these traits:

- **Simple UX** — if it's confusing, nobody recommends it (they'd have to explain it)
- **Clear value prop** — one sentence explains what it does and why it matters
- **Aesthetic design** — beautiful products get shared because they reflect taste
- **Unique functionality** — does something no competitor does (or does it dramatically better)
- **Makes users look cool** — the product becomes a status signal or identity marker

WOM is not luck. It's a design decision. If your product isn't generating WOM, one of these five is broken.

## Engineering Virality

### Jonah Berger's Research (Expanded)

Three pillars for engineered virality:

1. **Social Currency** — Give users insider status, achievements, or knowledge they want to display. Foursquare badges. Duolingo streaks. GitHub contribution graphs.
2. **Emotional Triggers** — Content that produces high-arousal emotions gets shared 3x more. Awe and humor dominate. Craft the product experience to produce emotional peaks.
3. **Practical Utility** — "This saved me 4 hours" is the most powerful sharing trigger in B2B. Build products so useful that NOT sharing them with colleagues feels selfish.

### Storytelling as Distribution

- Embed your product in a story people want to tell
- Blendtec "Will It Blend?" — the story is entertaining, the product is the vehicle
- The product must be inseparable from the narrative — if you can tell the story without mentioning the product, the integration failed

## Morning Brew Case Study

- **100K to 1.5M subscribers in 18 months**
- 420K subscribers came directly from word-of-mouth referrals (28% of total growth)

### Gamified Referral Tiers

Subscribers earned rewards at milestones:

| Referrals | Reward |
|-----------|--------|
| 1 | Sunday edition access |
| 3 | Stickers |
| 5 | T-shirt |
| 10 | Mug |
| 15 | Hoodie |
| 25 | Morning Brew branded gear set |

### Why It Worked

- Rewards were **aspirational and visible** — wearing the merch signals tribe membership
- Each tier was achievable enough to maintain motivation
- Progress was displayed prominently in every email — constant reminder
- The newsletter itself was shareable content — forwarding was natural behavior
- Low-cost rewards (merch) drove enormous acquisition value per subscriber
