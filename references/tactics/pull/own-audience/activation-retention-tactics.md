# Activation & Retention Tactics — Landing Pages, Onboarding, Gamification, Pricing, Analytics

Source: Bronson Taylor, Growth Hacking Playbook Modules 4-5

---

## Landing Pages

### Core Principle

A landing page has **one purpose: convert**. Every element either supports conversion or is a distraction to remove.

### Conversion-Centered Design

- **Headlines are the most important element** — 80% of visitors read the headline, only 20% read the body. Test headlines before anything else.
- **Social proof above the fold** — testimonials, user counts, press logos, star ratings. Proof reduces perceived risk.
- **Trust signals** — security badges, money-back guarantees, privacy statements. Remove friction from the decision.
- **Scarcity** — limited availability, countdown timers, "X people viewing this now." Real scarcity only — fake scarcity destroys trust.
- **Single CTA** — one action per page. Multiple CTAs split attention and reduce conversion. If you have two goals, build two pages.
- Remove navigation links — every exit point is a leak in your funnel

## Copywriting

> "The closest thing to magic in growth hacking."

### Length Matching

- **Short form for small asks** — email signup, free trial, low-cost product. Get in, state the value, get the click.
- **Long form for big asks** — enterprise sales, high-ticket products, complex decisions. More objections = more copy needed to overcome them.
- **Critical rule: if you write long copy for a low-cost product, you talk them OUT of buying.** You introduce doubts they didn't have. A $9 ebook needs a headline and three bullets, not a 2,000-word sales letter.

### Copy Hierarchy

1. Headline (what's in it for me?)
2. Subhead (how does it deliver?)
3. Bullets (specific benefits, not features)
4. Proof (who else got results?)
5. CTA (what do I do next?)

## CTAs (Calls to Action)

### Sticky CTAs

- Persist on scroll — fixed headers, floating buttons, sticky bars
- User should never have to scroll to find the action — it follows them

### Surroundings > Color

- The area around the CTA matters more than the button color itself
- White space, directional cues (arrows, eye gaze in images), and contrast with surrounding elements drive clicks
- Stop A/B testing green vs. orange — test what's around the button

### First-Person Pronouns

- **"Get My Free Trial"** > "Start Free Trial"
- **"Send Me the Guide"** > "Download Now"
- First-person language increases ownership feeling and conversion rates
- This is one of the highest-ROI copy changes — takes 5 seconds, lifts conversion measurably

### Positioning

- Place CTAs near the most compelling content (after a testimonial, after a key benefit, after a demo)
- Repeat the CTA at natural decision points — don't make users scroll back up
- Above the fold for aware traffic, below proof sections for cold traffic

## Onboarding

### Get to the "Aha Moment" ASAP

- The "aha moment" is when the user first experiences the product's core value
- Every step between signup and aha moment is a dropout risk
- Minimize steps. Remove optional fields. Defer account setup until after the value is delivered.

### Twitter's "Follow 5-10 Accounts" Hack

- New users who didn't follow anyone churned almost immediately — empty feed = no value
- Twitter inserted a step: "Follow at least 5-10 accounts" with curated suggestions
- This single intervention dramatically improved activation — the aha moment (interesting feed) now happened on day 1 instead of never

### Dropbox's Onboarding Referral

- Embedded referral as the **final onboarding step** — after the user had installed, synced a file, and experienced the core value
- Timing was everything: user hits referral prompt at peak excitement
- Positioned as benefit to the user ("Get more free space") not a favor to Dropbox

### Onboarding Principles

1. Show, don't tell — interactive walkthroughs beat documentation
2. Progressive disclosure — reveal features as users need them, not all at once
3. Celebrate micro-wins — progress bars, checkmarks, congratulations screens
4. Default to populated — empty states kill engagement (pre-fill with sample data, templates, or suggestions)

## Gamification

### Audible: Badges and Streaks

- Listening badges and streaks increase LTV by encouraging habitual use
- Streaks create loss aversion — users return to avoid breaking the streak
- Badges provide social currency — shareable achievements

### Hotel Tonight Perks Program

- **3x better conversion** among Perks members vs. non-members
- **7x more bookings** from loyalty tier users
- **22% retention increase** from gamified loyalty mechanics

### Implementation Principles

- Gamification must align with core product behavior — reward what you want users to do more of
- Points/badges without meaning become noise — tie rewards to real value (discounts, features, access)
- Leaderboards work for competitive audiences, backfire for casual users
- Start simple: one mechanic (streaks OR points OR tiers), validate, then layer

## Pricing Strategy

### Decoy Effect (Movie Theatre Pricing)

- Small: $5 | Medium: $7.50 | Large: $8
- Nobody buys the medium — it exists to make the large look like a bargain
- Apply to SaaS tiers: the middle plan should make the top plan look obvious

### Price Anchoring

- Show the most expensive option first
- Once the user sees $999/mo, $299/mo feels reasonable — even if $299 was the "expensive" option before anchoring
- Works on pricing pages, in sales conversations, in proposals

### Core Truth

> "Pricing is arbitrary — test, don't guess."

- Most founders set prices based on cost-plus or competitor matching — both are guesses
- Run pricing experiments: different cohorts see different prices
- Willingness-to-pay varies wildly by segment, positioning, and framing
- A 20% price increase with 5% churn loss is almost always net positive

## Retention — The "Elite 8" Principles

### The Economics

- **5% retention increase = 25-95% profit increase** (Bain & Company)
- Retention is the highest-leverage growth metric — it compounds everything else
- A leaky bucket makes all acquisition spend wasteful

### The Eight Principles

1. **Design switching costs from day one** — integrations, data lock-in, workflow dependencies, social graphs. The more embedded your product becomes, the harder it is to leave.
2. **Measure NPS religiously** — Net Promoter Score segments users into Promoters (9-10), Passives (7-8), and Detractors (0-6). Detractors churn and damage brand. Promoters refer and expand.
3. **86% of customers willing to pay more for better experience** — retention is a CX problem, not a pricing problem. Invest in support, UX, speed.
4. **Reactivation campaigns** — target churned users with win-back offers. They already know the product; the barrier to return is lower than the barrier to acquire new.
5. **Cohort analysis** — track retention by signup cohort, not aggregate. Aggregate metrics hide decay. If Cohort 3 retains worse than Cohort 1, something changed.
6. **Engagement scoring** — define "healthy" usage patterns and flag users who deviate. Intervene before they churn, not after.
7. **Feature adoption tracking** — users who adopt 3+ features churn at half the rate of single-feature users. Drive feature discovery through onboarding, emails, and in-app prompts.
8. **Customer success, not customer support** — proactively help users achieve outcomes, don't wait for tickets. Support is reactive; success is preventive.

## Growth Experiments

### Scientific Method for Growth

1. **Question** — "Why is our trial-to-paid conversion at 8%?"
2. **Hypothesis** — "Users who don't complete onboarding within 24 hours convert at 2%. If we send a reminder at hour 12, conversion will increase."
3. **Prediction** — "12-hour reminder will lift trial-to-paid by 15%."
4. **Testing** — A/B test: 50% get reminder, 50% don't. Run until statistically significant.
5. **Analysis** — Did the prediction hold? What else changed? What's the next experiment?

### ICE Framework for Prioritization

Score every experiment idea 1-10 on three dimensions:

- **Impact** — If this works, how much will it move the needle?
- **Confidence** — How sure are we this will work? (data > intuition > gut)
- **Ease** — How fast and cheap can we run this test?

Multiply scores. Run highest ICE experiments first. Kill low-ICE ideas ruthlessly.

### Experiment Discipline

- **99% confidence level** — don't call a winner at 80% significance. False positives waste resources.
- **"Control wins in a tie"** — if results are ambiguous, keep the current version. Change requires clear evidence.
- Document every experiment: hypothesis, setup, result, learnings. Build institutional knowledge.
- Run one experiment per funnel stage at a time — overlapping tests contaminate results.

## Analytics

### Growth Pyramid

Build analytics from the bottom up:

1. **Data** (foundation) — clean, reliable tracking. Events, properties, user identity. If the data is wrong, every decision built on it is wrong.
2. **Traffic** — sources, volumes, quality by channel. Know where users come from and what they do differently by source.
3. **Conversion** — funnel metrics at every stage. Identify the biggest drop-off and fix that first.
4. **Retention** (top) — cohort curves, engagement frequency, churn prediction. This is the metric that determines whether the business compounds or decays.

### North Star Metric

- One metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers
- Airbnb: Nights Booked. Slack: Daily Active Users sending messages. Spotify: Time Spent Listening.
- Every team's work should ladder up to the North Star. If an initiative doesn't move it, question whether it matters.

### Viral Coefficient

- K = (invites sent per user) x (conversion rate of invites)
- **K > 1 = exponential growth** — each user brings in more than one new user
- K = 0.5 is still valuable — it means your paid acquisition is 50% cheaper because half your users come free
- Optimize both sides: increase invites sent AND increase invite conversion rate

### Critical Rule

> **Establish analytics BEFORE acquiring users.**

- If you can't measure it, you can't optimize it
- Launching acquisition campaigns without analytics is burning money in the dark
- Minimum viable analytics: event tracking, funnel visualization, cohort retention, source attribution

## Product-Market Fit

### Seed Growth (Revisited)

- The layer beneath PMF — manual, unscalable user acquisition to validate demand
- You need users to test PMF, but you can't scale acquisition without PMF
- Seed Growth breaks the chicken-and-egg: get 50-100 users through brute force, then measure fit

### Sean Ellis 40% Test

Ask existing users: **"How would you feel if you could no longer use this product?"**

- **40%+ say "very disappointed"** = you have Product-Market Fit
- Below 40% = you don't. Stop scaling. Fix the product.
- This is the single most reliable PMF signal — more predictive than revenue, retention, or NPS in early-stage companies

### The Ultimate Prerequisite

> **Without Product-Market Fit, every tactic in this playbook is wasted.**

- Paid ads amplify a broken product into faster failure
- Viral loops on a product nobody wants just accelerate negative word-of-mouth
- Retention hacks on a product that doesn't solve a real problem are band-aids on a bullet wound
- PMF first. Scale second. Always.
