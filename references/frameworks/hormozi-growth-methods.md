# Hormozi Growth Methods

Extracted from Alex Hormozi's 400+ YouTube Lessons. Each entry is a named growth pattern with definition, trigger, mechanics, and formula where applicable.

---

## 1. The Value Equation

**Definition:** A formula for engineering perceived offer value by maximizing upside and minimizing friction.

**Trigger:** When an offer feels "meh" or conversion is low despite real demand.

**Mechanics:**
- Increase the **Dream Outcome** -- make the result vivid, specific, and emotionally meaningful (dollars, hours saved, status gained)
- Increase **Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** -- stack proof, testimonials, case studies, and guarantees
- Decrease **Time Delay** -- design fast wins in the first 24-72 hours so buyers feel progress immediately
- Decrease **Effort & Sacrifice** -- add DFY support, templates, simpler steps, and better tools
- Address hidden costs (time, reputation risk, emotional risk) explicitly in your pitch

**Formula:**
```
Perceived Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

**Source:** Lessons 227-232

## 2. Grand Slam Offer

**Definition:** One flagship offer so outrageously valuable that it becomes the thing your brand is known for -- the anchor of your entire acquisition and retention engine.

**Trigger:** When you're juggling multiple mediocre offers, or your core offer doesn't generate unprompted word-of-mouth.

**Mechanics:**
- Define the single most painful, valuable problem for your best customers
- Stack every practical advantage around solving it: clear promise, strong proof, fast-win onboarding, smart guarantees, helpful bonuses, robust delivery
- Apply the "feel stupid saying no" test: would a rational person feel like they're making a mistake by declining?
- Over-deliver at first to generate powerful case studies, then systematize
- Build your entire acquisition, upsell, and referral engine around this one core offer

**Formula:**
```
Grand Slam = Big Outcome + High Certainty + Fast Result + Low Effort + Risk Reversal
```

**Source:** Lessons 233-234

## 3. The "Feel Stupid Saying No" Test

**Definition:** An offer design litmus test -- if the prospect isn't thinking "I'd be dumb not to try this," the value stack isn't asymmetric enough.

**Trigger:** Before launching or repricing any offer. Use as a final sanity check.

**Mechanics:**
- Stack clear benefits until the value-to-price ratio is heavily tilted in the buyer's favor
- Ensure the promised outcome is significant AND specific
- Support likelihood with proof and guarantees
- Minimize time to value, effort required, and hidden costs
- Add bonuses that fill remaining gaps (tools, templates, support they'd buy elsewhere)
- When done right, the price -- even premium -- feels small compared to the combined gain and removed risk

**Source:** Lesson 234

## 4. More, Better, New (Advertising & Innovation Sequence)

**Definition:** The priority sequence for growth: first do MORE of what works, then make it BETTER, then try something NEW.

**Trigger:** When something is working even a little and you feel the urge to chase a new shiny object.

**Mechanics:**
- **More:** Increase volume on what's already working -- more ad spend on a profitable campaign, more outreach with a proven script, more traffic to a converting page
- **Better:** Optimize -- improve funnels, dial messaging, reduce churn, raise prices, refine onboarding
- **New:** Only after you've reasonably saturated and optimized existing winners, divert resources into new offers, markets, or channels
- Following this sequence prevents spreading thin; "new" is deliberately placed last, not first

**Formula:**
```
Growth Priority = More (existing winners) --> Better (optimize) --> New (experiment)
```

**Source:** Lesson 246

## 5. The Rule of 100

**Definition:** Commit to 100 meaningful acquisition actions per day to turn selling from a hope game into a volume game with real data.

**Trigger:** When sales feel unpredictable, emotional, or when you lack data to optimize.

**Mechanics:**
- 100 units can be: outbound messages, cold emails, calls, dollars of ad spend, or minutes of content creation
- The exact mix matters less than daily volume and consistency
- At this scale, real patterns emerge: which hooks get replies, which audiences click, which offers convert
- Volume doesn't replace strategy -- it exposes reality and gives you data to iterate
- If 100/day for a month yields nothing, the script or target is definitively broken

**Formula:**
```
100 actions/day x 30 days = 3,000 data points --> real signal, not noise
```

**Source:** Lesson 269

## 6. Volume x Leverage Model

**Definition:** Scale output by combining high volume of inputs with increasing leverage (better offers, systems, teams, content, capital).

**Trigger:** When you've hit a ceiling on personal effort and need to grow without linearly adding hours.

**Mechanics:**
- Start with high personal volume (Rule of 100) to learn what works
- Add leverage through: better offers (higher close rate per conversation), systems (automation, SOPs), teams (trained reps, setters, closers), content (trust at scale), and capital (paid ads amplifying proven funnels)
- Each layer of leverage multiplies the output of the same volume of inputs
- Convert founder tasks into projects, processes, or people (Lesson 216)
- Layer acquisition channels over time: owned, earned, paid, people-based

**Source:** Lessons 216, 269, 281, 283

## 7. LTGP:CAC Ratio

**Definition:** The ratio of Lifetime Gross Profit per customer to Customer Acquisition Cost -- the single most important constraint on how aggressively you can grow.

**Trigger:** Before scaling ad spend, hiring sales reps, or opening new channels. Also when diagnosing why growth feels unprofitable.

**Mechanics:**
- Calculate Lifetime Gross Profit: Lifetime Revenue per Customer minus Lifetime COGS per Customer
- Calculate CAC: total acquisition spend (ads, commissions, sales team cost) divided by customers acquired
- Target LTGP:CAC of at least 3:1 (for every $1 spent acquiring, earn $3 in gross profit)
- Also measure 30-Day Cash:CAC -- aim for 1:1 or better so acquisition is self-financing
- If LTGP:CAC is weak, fix LTV/margins BEFORE increasing ad spend

**Formula:**
```
LTGP:CAC = (Lifetime Revenue - Lifetime COGS) / Customer Acquisition Cost
Target: >= 3:1

30-Day Cash:CAC = Cash collected in first 30 days / CAC
Target: >= 1:1
```

**Source:** Lessons 248, 251-253

## 8. LTV Growth Levers

**Definition:** Four specific levers that drive Lifetime Value -- work them deliberately instead of hoping LTV grows on its own.

**Trigger:** When LTV is flat, churn is high, or you need to justify higher acquisition spend.

**Mechanics:**
- **Retention:** Fix onboarding, set clear expectations, proactively manage early churn. If customers leave quickly, nothing else matters.
- **Frequency:** Create natural reasons to buy again -- renewals, seasonal cycles, campaigns, complementary services
- **Pricing:** Raise prices where value supports it. Introduce premium tiers, value-based pricing, or prepay discounts
- **Ascension:** Design "next step" offers for best customers -- higher-touch programs, DFY implementation, masterminds, advisory

**Formula:**
```
LTV = Average Revenue per Period x (1 / Churn Rate)
Profit LTV = LTV x Gross Margin %
```

**Source:** Lessons 250, 259, 370-371

## 9. The 100 Customers Thought Experiment

**Definition:** Focus on getting your first 100 customers with one avatar, one product, one channel -- before expanding anything.

**Trigger:** Pre-$1M/year. When tempted to diversify too early or add complexity.

**Mechanics:**
- Choose ONE specific avatar ("gym owners with 2-5 locations"), ONE clear outcome, ONE offer, ONE acquisition channel
- Hyper-focus gives speed: scripts, content, onboarding all aim at one target
- Collect testimonials that all speak to the same result
- With fewer variables, you quickly see what works and what's broken
- Only expand (upmarket, downmarket, adjacent, new channels) after you dominate the niche

**Formula:**
```
1 Avatar + 1 Product + 1 Channel --> First 100 customers --> Then expand
```

**Source:** Lessons 224-225, 268

## 10. Content as Trust at Scale

**Definition:** Content is "giving" at scale -- it builds trust with thousands simultaneously so that when you "ask" (ads/offers), the audience is pre-sold.

**Trigger:** When you need to warm audiences beyond 1:1 conversations, or when paid ads feel expensive because prospects don't know you.

**Mechanics:**
- Treat content as "give" and ads as "ask" -- lead with generous value
- Short-form content = cold reach engine (grab attention, curiosity, shareability)
- Long-form content = proof and conversion engine (frameworks, case studies, personality)
- Classify every piece by outcome: legitimacy, nurture, or acquisition
- Test hooks and angles organically before paying for ads
- Repurpose: one long-form piece becomes 10-20 touchpoints across platforms
- Optimize for business metrics (leads, revenue, LTV), not vanity metrics (likes, views)

**Source:** Lessons 326, 329, 339, 345-347

## 11. Give Away the Secrets, Sell the Implementation

**Definition:** Share your methods, frameworks, and knowledge freely in content -- then charge for the done-with-you or done-for-you execution of those methods.

**Trigger:** When debating how much to share for free vs. gate behind a paywall.

**Mechanics:**
- Free content solves specific, real problems -- builds trust and positions you as the obvious expert
- The "secrets" (what to do) are freely available; the "implementation" (how to do it with support, speed, and accountability) is the paid product
- Lead magnets solve one tiny, specific problem to start the relationship
- Content should naturally set up your core offer -- give a piece of the journey, not the whole path
- People who consume free content and get wins become the warmest, highest-converting buyers

**Source:** Lessons 271, 326, 330, 343

## 12. The Choreographed Funnel

**Definition:** A designed sequence that turns strangers into clients through escalating value and commitment at each step.

**Trigger:** When acquisition feels random or you can't diagnose where prospects drop off.

**Mechanics:**
- **Ad/Content** grabs attention with a simple promise
- **Lead Magnet** delivers a quick win in exchange for contact info
- **VSL** (Video Sales Letter) educates, pre-handles objections, and builds belief
- **Qualified Call** (via application + calendar) confirms fit and presents the offer
- **Offer** with anchored price, step-down options, and payment plans
- Track each step: click --> opt-in --> book --> show --> close --> retained
- Fix the weakest conversion point before scaling input volume

**Formula:**
```
Funnel Metrics:
Impressions --> Clicks --> Opt-ins --> Booked --> Showed --> Closed --> Retained
Each transition has a conversion rate. Optimize the weakest link first.
```

**Source:** Lessons 274-275

## 13. Price Anchoring & Step-Down

**Definition:** Present the highest-value option first, then offer step-down alternatives -- so the core offer feels accessible by comparison.

**Trigger:** When presenting pricing on sales calls or pricing pages.

**Mechanics:**
- Present full, outcome-based value first (e.g., "Private consulting to achieve this would run $30,000+")
- Introduce your structured program at a lower number (e.g., "$10,000")
- Offer step-down options: full-pay discount, installment plans (slightly higher total), or lower-support versions
- Prepay discounts reward commitment and improve your cash flow
- Frame payment plans as tools to ACCESS the outcome, not as discounts

**Source:** Lessons 237, 298, 301

## 14. Conditional Guarantees (Risk Reversal)

**Definition:** Shift perceived risk off the buyer with guarantees tied to specific customer actions -- increasing conversion while filtering for serious buyers.

**Trigger:** When prospects hesitate due to fear of wasting money, or when you need to differentiate from competitors.

**Mechanics:**
- Design guarantees with a clear "or what" -- refund, extended service, free rework, credit
- Tie conditions to behaviors that predict success: attendance, implementation, usage milestones
- Make conditions clear and trackable (screenshots, call attendance, reports)
- Layer guarantees: short unconditional window (7-14 days) + longer conditional guarantee tied to implementation
- Run the numbers: what % might claim, what's the cost, can operations handle it?
- Bold guarantees become a competitive moat when your operations are solid

**Source:** Lessons 239-241, 302, 373

## 15. The Delivery Cube

**Definition:** A multi-dimensional framework for designing how your offer is delivered -- adjust axes to match different segments and economics.

**Trigger:** When deciding between high-touch and scalable models, or when margins are squeezed by over-customization.

**Mechanics:**
- Five axes: Attention (1:1 vs group), Personalization (custom vs standardized), Medium (live vs recorded vs text), Speed (instant vs delayed support), Price
- High-ticket: high attention, high personalization, live, fast response, premium price
- Scalable: group-based, templated, recorded, slower support, lower price
- Map your current services to reveal gaps and opportunities
- Consciously decide between DIY, Done-With-You, and Done-For-You models
- Use service-delivery levers (group vs 1:1, channels, speed) to scale without killing value

**Source:** Lessons 242-244

## 16. Nail It Before You Scale It

**Definition:** Do not amplify (with ads, hires, or channels) until the unit economics, delivery model, and offer are proven at small scale.

**Trigger:** When tempted to pour money into ads or hire reps before the core model is validated.

**Mechanics:**
- Validate with meaningful volume: 20-50 sales calls with the same script, or a set minimum ad spend, before judging
- Ensure incremental gross margin per unit is strong (70-80%+ for services)
- Fulfillment process must be documented, repeatable, and not entirely dependent on you
- Test organically or with small ad spends first -- see consistent satisfaction and retention
- Fix business model math (margins, LTV, churn) BEFORE throwing more people, software, or ads at the problem
- Scaling a weak model just amplifies problems faster

**Source:** Lessons 247, 256, 265, 268

## 17. BAMFAM (Book A Meeting From A Meeting)

**Definition:** Always schedule the next interaction before the current one ends -- eliminates ghosting and maintains momentum.

**Trigger:** Every sales call, discovery call, onboarding session, and check-in.

**Mechanics:**
- Before hanging up, say: "Whether you move forward or not, the next logical step is X. Does Tuesday at 3 or Wednesday at 11 work better?"
- Avoid vague "I'll follow up" or "I'll send some info"
- Extend to referrals: at point of close, ask "Who's one person you know who would benefit from this?"
- Build into every role (setter, closer, CSM)
- Reduces ghosts, shortens sales cycles, creates a pipeline of scheduled commitments

**Source:** Lessons 305, 312

## 18. Acknowledge, Associate, Ask (Objection Handling Loop)

**Definition:** A three-step loop for defusing sales objections without creating conflict.

**Trigger:** When a prospect raises a concern, hesitation, or objection during a sales conversation.

**Mechanics:**
- **Acknowledge:** Validate the concern ("I hear you -- this is a significant investment")
- **Associate:** Normalize and reframe ("A lot of our best clients felt the same way initially...")
- **Ask:** Dig deeper ("Can you tell me more about what specifically worries you?" or "If we solved that, would anything else hold you back?")
- Loop as many times as needed for different objections
- Build a library of strong associations (stories, analogies, reframes) to plug into the middle step

**Source:** Lesson 297

## 19. Four Core Acquisition Methods

**Definition:** Almost all customer acquisition rolls up into four methods. Master one before adding others.

**Trigger:** When acquisition feels chaotic or you're chasing too many tactics simultaneously.

**Mechanics:**
- **Warm Outreach:** DMs, emails, calls to people who already know you -- fastest early traction
- **Content:** Posts, videos, podcasts, emails -- slow to build but compounds over time
- **Paid Ads:** Buying attention on platforms -- scalable but requires cash and skill
- **Partners:** Affiliates, agencies, JVs -- high leverage but demands trust and proof
- Master one channel (repeatable, documented) before adding a second
- Layer over time: owned (email, SMS) --> earned (organic, SEO) --> paid (ads) --> people-based (affiliates, reps)
- Always maintain at least one reliable backup channel

**Source:** Lessons 266-267, 281-282

## 20. Lead Magnet Design

**Definition:** A small, specific, free asset that solves one micro-problem to start a relationship and capture contact info.

**Trigger:** When you need to convert anonymous attention into known contacts (top of funnel).

**Mechanics:**
- Solve a TINY, specific problem your ideal customer actually feels right now
- Keep it short and immediately actionable: calculator, checklist, 3-step template, 10-min video
- The magnet should naturally set up your core offer (not be random/unrelated)
- Use opt-in qualifiers (revenue range, role, timeline) to filter for serious buyers
- Front-end "free" or low-ticket offers ($27-$97) can liquidate some or all CAC
- Design a smooth path from lead magnet --> nurture --> core product

**Source:** Lessons 271-272, 355

## 21. The Referral Engine

**Definition:** A systematic approach to turning every satisfied customer into a reliable source of new business.

**Trigger:** When acquisition costs are rising or you want a compounding channel that doesn't depend on ad platforms.

**Mechanics:**
- Ask EVERY satisfied customer for referrals -- use specific prompts, not vague "know anyone?"
- Time the ask at moments of peak success and emotional high (after a win, a milestone, positive feedback)
- Provide easy mechanics: pre-written intro messages, referral links, 3-way text intros, simple forms
- Design incentives that match your economics (credits, discounts, gifts, early access)
- Track which incentives and formats actually convert, then double down
- Invest in helping customers reach wins faster -- better outcomes drive more word-of-mouth
- Referrals compound: if referral rate outpaces churn, you can grow without increasing ad spend

**Source:** Lessons 276-280

## 22. Activation-Based Onboarding

**Definition:** Design onboarding backwards from the activation moment -- the first experience of real value -- and compress time to get there.

**Trigger:** When churn is high in the first 30-60 days, or when customers buy but never fully engage.

**Mechanics:**
- Define activation: the specific behavior or result that predicts long-term retention (first campaign launched, first client booked, first key setup completed)
- Track activation rate as a central KPI
- Design every onboarding email, call, video, and prompt to shepherd customers to that first "aha" in 24-48 hours
- Use high-touch 48-hour onboarding (live call, personalized plan, co-created 7-14 day roadmap) for higher-ticket offers
- Assign named points of contact for relationship and accountability
- Monitor consumption, attendance, and usage as early predictors of churn
- Escalate outreach when engagement drops -- intervene BEFORE cancellation

**Source:** Lessons 363-368

## 23. The Value Ladder

**Definition:** A sequence of products/services at progressively higher price points that meets customers at each stage of their journey.

**Trigger:** When best customers are outgrowing your offer and have nowhere to go, or when LTV is capped by a single product.

**Mechanics:**
- Entry-level: lead magnet or low-ticket offer solves the initial problem and builds trust
- Core offer: the Grand Slam Offer that delivers the primary transformation
- Backend/Ascension: masterminds, advanced trainings, DFY services, higher-tier retainers for customers who want more
- Each step matches increased customer sophistication with increased value and investment
- Repackage one-off deliverables into outcome-based programs and continuity
- Treat recurring revenue as a continuum to improve, not a binary

**Source:** Lessons 245, 260, 371

## 24. Email as Owned Media Engine

**Definition:** Treat your email list as the highest-ROI channel you own -- it's platform-independent, near-zero marginal cost, and compounds.

**Trigger:** When you're over-reliant on rented platforms (social, ads) or sitting on underused contact lists.

**Mechanics:**
- Leverage existing contacts BEFORE re-buying attention with ads -- past buyers, old leads, event attendees, cart abandoners
- Use simple, text-based templates optimized for mobile
- One clear CTA per email, always include a PS
- Track revenue per send as the ultimate metric (not just opens/clicks)
- Segment by behavior and interest for relevance
- Batch-write emails and maintain a consistent cadence
- Encourage replies to improve deliverability and build relationship
- Turn testimonials and "worst moment" stories into compelling email hooks
- Install pixels and use cross-platform retargeting to follow engaged users

**Source:** Lessons 349-360

## 25. Retention-First Profit Multiplier

**Definition:** Retention is the highest-leverage lever for profit and valuation -- improving it multiplies the effect of every other lever.

**Trigger:** When churn is above 5% monthly, or when acquisition costs are rising faster than revenue.

**Mechanics:**
- Measure churn properly: by cohort, M12 retention, averaged over months
- Track activation metrics and design onboarding around them
- Use consumption, attendance, and usage as early predictors of churn
- Escalate outreach when engagement drops (email --> DM --> loom --> phone call)
- Commission customer success teams based on outcomes and retention, not just "keeping people happy"
- Use term contracts, switching costs, and stored value (data, assets, automations) to stick customers ethically
- Reactivate churned customers with personalized outreach addressing the original reason they left
- Fix systemic product/onboarding issues rather than blaming "bad customers"

**Formula:**
```
If avg customer pays $200/mo and stays 6 months: LTV = $1,200
Improve retention to 12 months: LTV = $2,400 (doubled, same customer base)
That extra profit funds better product, stronger team, more marketing.
```

**Source:** Lessons 361-376

## 26. Market Selection Filter

**Definition:** Four filters for choosing a market where your effort is multiplied, not wasted.

**Trigger:** Before starting a new business or offer, or when current market feels structurally difficult.

**Mechanics:**
- **Real Pain:** Are people desperate to solve this, or is it "nice to have"? Look for complaints, workarounds, money already being spent
- **Ability to Pay:** Can this group afford a price that makes your business viable?
- **Easy Findability:** Can you locate these people? Do they gather in specific places (events, groups, tools, job titles)?
- **Growth:** Is the segment expanding, stable, or shrinking?
- Avoid markets with structural churn (customers constantly going out of business) or low purchasing power
- The right market has natural demand -- you spend energy helping motivated people choose YOU, not making people care

**Source:** Lessons 222-223, 226

## 27. The Scientific Method for Business

**Definition:** Run your business like a lab: Goal --> Hypothesis --> Implementation --> Outcome.

**Trigger:** When making any significant change to offers, pricing, funnels, or operations.

**Mechanics:**
- Clarify the goal with a number and timeframe ("Increase booked calls by 30% in 60 days")
- State a testable hypothesis ("If we add a video to the booking page, more people will complete the form")
- Implement with a defined window, protecting from other conflicting changes
- Measure outcome against baseline
- Use data -- not hope -- to decide whether to push, pause, or pivot
- Distinguish good-hard (solvable with iterations) from bad-hard (structurally unsound)

**Source:** Lessons 207-209

## 28. Funnel Diagnostics by Data

**Definition:** Walk through your funnel step-by-step using numbers to diagnose exactly where the drop-off happens -- never by vibes.

**Trigger:** When results dip and you're tempted to blame leads, the market, or "no one has money."

**Mechanics:**
- Track: leads in --> booked --> showed --> offered --> closed --> cash collected --> retained
- Compare ratios to past performance or benchmarks
- Low show rate? Not a closing problem -- fix reminders or qualification
- Many calls but few offers? Discovery or targeting is off
- Offers made but few close? Work on belief, framing, objections
- Close but don't retain? Delivery and onboarding are the leverage points
- Run controlled experiments: change one variable, watch the corresponding metric

**Formula:**
```
Revenue = Leads x Book Rate x Show Rate x Close Rate x Avg Price
Each conversion rate is a diagnostic lever.
```

**Source:** Lessons 275, 304

## 29. Cash Acceleration Design

**Definition:** Engineer your offers to pull cash forward -- collect more money earlier in the relationship to fund growth without external capital.

**Trigger:** When cash flow is tight despite being profitable on paper, or when you need to scale acquisition faster.

**Mechanics:**
- Prepay discounts: reward full payment with meaningful discount or bonus
- Uneven payment splits: higher first payment followed by lower installments
- Front-end low-ticket offers ($27-$97) that liquidate CAC
- Bundles: package multiple services/periods at attractive combined price
- Credits: customers pre-buy a bank of sessions/deliverables
- Track first-30-day cash per customer as a core metric
- Strengthen 30-day Cash:CAC ratio to make acquisition self-financing

**Source:** Lessons 262, 272, 301

## 30. Document, Demonstrate, Duplicate (Training Framework)

**Definition:** A systematic method for transferring knowledge from founder to team that actually sticks.

**Trigger:** When you need to train people on any repeatable process, especially during scaling.

**Mechanics:**
- **Document:** Write clear steps, templates, examples, and definitions of "done" -- turns personal know-how into visible, repeatable process
- **Demonstrate:** Walk through the task live (screen share, narrate thinking, show edge cases) -- bridges gap between static instructions and real execution
- **Duplicate:** Trainee performs while you observe and give real-time feedback -- repeat until they execute independently to standard
- Reuse assets: new hires watch documentation and demos, then move quickly into duplication
- Over time, your library of documented processes becomes a core business asset

**Source:** Lesson 390

## 31. The Management Diamond

**Definition:** A diagnostic framework for missed work -- run through five dimensions before blaming the person.

**Trigger:** When someone drops the ball or performance lags.

**Mechanics:**
- **Expectation:** Did they clearly know what "done" looked like?
- **Skill:** Did they know how to do it at the required level?
- **Motivation:** Did they care enough? Did they see why it mattered?
- **Blockers:** Was anything in their way (missing info, access, approvals, competing tasks)?
- **When:** Did they know the deadline and its priority relative to everything else?
- Walk through in order. Apply the corresponding fix: clarify, train, motivate, unblock, or re-prioritize.

**Source:** Lesson 378

## 32. Enterprise Value Growth Formula

**Definition:** View major business decisions through their impact on enterprise value, not just monthly revenue.

**Trigger:** When evaluating significant investments, hires, or strategic moves -- especially if eventual exit is a consideration.

**Mechanics:**
- Enterprise Value = EBITDA x Multiple
- Four levers increase valuation multiple: better retention, more predictable revenue, higher margins, lower key-man risk
- A project adding $500K annual EBITDA at 6x multiple = $3M enterprise value created
- Design for these simultaneously: retention (onboarding, product), predictability (subscriptions, contracts), margins (price up, COGS down), independence (systems, teams)
- Build "scale zero" -- the business can operate at current size without founder daily involvement

**Formula:**
```
Enterprise Value = EBITDA x Multiple
Multiple increases with: retention, predictability, margins, low key-man risk
```

**Source:** Lessons 214, 416-417
